Lens 1: Euclidean Distance (for Numeric Data)

Measures the straight-line "as the crow flies" distance between two points. It is highly sensitive to the magnitude (scale) of the features.

Unscaled Data Distance ≈ 2.037

This result was misleadingly driven almost entirely by the `people_count` feature.

Scaled Data Distance ≈ 1.732

Gives a balanced contribution from all features, making the comparison meaningful.

Key Point: If you use Euclidean distance, you **must** scale your numeric features first. Otherwise, you're not comparing content, you're just comparing units.

Lens 2: Cosine Similarity (for Numeric Data)

Measures the cosine of the angle ($ \theta $) between two vectors, ignoring their magnitude. It tells us if they point in the same direction.

On Raw Vectors: Similarity ≈ 0.951

Deceptively high. The large `people_count` value forces both vectors to point in a roughly similar direction.

On Normalized Vectors: Similarity = 0.0

The vectors are orthogonal (90° angle), correctly identifying that their feature emphases are completely different.

Key Point: Cosine similarity is excellent for comparing the proportions of features, not their absolute scales.

Lens 3: Hamming Distance (for Categorical Data)

A simple and intuitive metric that counts the number of positions where labels are different.

Feature 'scene': home ≠ stage → Mismatch = 1

Feature 'angle': side ≠ front/oblique → Mismatch = 1

Total Hamming Distance = 2

Key Point: Hamming distance is an "all or nothing" comparison. It's useful but can be brittle if labels are noisy or inconsistent.

Lens 4: Jaccard Similarity (for Sets)

The perfect metric for comparing sets, like our `instrument_types`. It measures the ratio of the intersection to the union of the sets.

L001
{guitar}
{guitar, drums}
R001

Intersection ({guitar} ∩ {guitar, drums}) → {guitar}, Size = 1

Union ({guitar} ∪ {guitar, drums}) → {guitar, drums}, Size = 2

Jaccard Similarity = 1 / 2 = 0.5

Key Point: This elegantly captures the story: "The clips share one instrument, but the union of all instruments between them is two."

Common Pitfalls: Know Your Metric's Weakness

Euclidean lies if you forget to scale your data.
Cosine can be misleading if one massive feature dominates the vector's direction.
Hamming is very sensitive to a single mislabeled category.
Jaccard can be punishing if some items in the set are more important than others.
