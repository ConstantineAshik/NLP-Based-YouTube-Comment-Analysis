Part 5: The "Surprise" Test (Chi-Square)

The Problem: Sometimes we see a pattern, but it's just a coincidence.

The Solution: The Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) test measures "How surprised should we be?" It compares what we Actually Saw (Observed) vs. what we Should Have Seen (Expected) if everything was random.

Scenario: Coffee & Cake

Imagine we track 100 customers. Does buying Coffee make someone more likely to buy Cake?

2. Wait, where does "Expected" come from?

This is the most critical part. The "Expected" number is calculated by assuming Independence (Randomness).

Expected Value = $\frac{\text{Row Total} \times \text{Column Total}}{\text{Grand Total}}$

Try it: In the grid above, notice that even as you change the observed numbers, the totals (Row/Col) stay constant at 50/50.
Since 50% of people bought Coffee, and 50% bought Cake, random chance says $0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.25$ (25 people) should buy both.

The Rule of Thumb:
If $\chi^2 > 3.84$, the result is Significant (p < 0.05).
This means the pattern is strong enough that it's probably NOT luck.
